AI Design Thinking Toolkit

For Educators & Facilitators

Module Overview: Building AI Teammates

This module moves beyond simple prompt engineering. It uses the Design Thinking Framework to teach students how to build AI assistants that solve real user problems. The core philosophy is treating the AI not just as a tool, but as a "teammate" in a continuous learning loop.

Why Empathy First?

Students often jump straight to coding or prompting. This curriculum forces a pause to ask: "Who is this for?" and "What is their pain point?" This ensures the technical solution is grounded in human need.

The Learning Loop

Encourage students to view the building process as a "continuous interview." They learn about their own blind spots by seeing how the AI interprets their instructions.

Suggested Lesson Flow

  • Step 1: The Mindset Shift (15 min): Discuss the "AI as Teammate" concept. Discuss why solutions fail when they lack empathy.
  • Step 2: Design Thinking Intro (20 min): Walk through the 5 phases: Empathy, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test.
  • Step 3: Worksheet Planning (30 min): Students must complete the Project Worksheet before touching any AI tool. This prevents aimless tinkering.
  • Step 4: The Build (45-60 min): Students use tools like Chatbase, Mizou, or Custom GPTs to implement their plan.
  • Step 5: Testing & Iteration (30 min): Students swap bots with a partner to "break" them and gather feedback.

Building Your AI Assistant

A Design Thinking Approach

1. The Mindset: Building a Teammate

You aren't just writing code or prompts. You are training a digital teammate. Approach this project with a Growth Mindset:

"View this process as a continuous interview. You discover how to talk to your AI to get better results, and you learn about your own blind spots in communication."

2. The Process: Design Thinking

1. Empathy
2. Define
3. Ideate
4. Prototype
5. Test
1

Empathy: Understand the User

Don't start with solutions. Start with people. Who is this bot for? What problem keeps them up at night? If the technical solution works but the user hates it, the project has failed.

2

Define: Frame the Problem

Create boundaries. What specifically will your assistant do? What will it NOT do? (e.g., "Do not give the answer, only a hint.")

3

Ideate: Brainstorm Solutions

Design the interaction. How should the assistant engage? (Professional or funny?) How should it start? What logical steps must it follow?

4

Prototype: Build the Bot

Use tools like Chatbase.co, Mizou, or Custom GPTs. Input your instructions and any knowledge files (PDFs) the bot needs.

5

Test & Iterate

Try to "break" your bot. Does it hallucinate? Does it get rude? Adjust your instructions and try again.

Required Documentation

You must submit documentation alongside your bot. Include:

  • Ideas & Insights: What did you learn about your user?
  • Decisions: Why did you choose this specific personality or tool?
  • Challenges: What went wrong? How did you fix it?
  • The Terms: A quick summary of what you learned from the tool's Privacy Statement.

Project Rubric

Criteria Excellent (10-8 pts) Competent (7-5 pts) Needs Improvement (4-0 pts)
Design Thinking Application Evidence of deep user empathy; Problem is clearly defined; Solution directly addresses the specific user pain point. User is identified but generic; Solution is functional but lacks specific tailoring to user needs. Little to no evidence of user consideration; "Solution looking for a problem."
Bot Functionality (Prototype) Bot adheres to guardrails; Persona is consistent; Handles inputs logically; effectively guides the user. Bot generally works but breaks character easily; Guardrails are weak or easily bypassed. Bot fails to load or does not perform the basic task requested.
Documentation (Process) Detailed log of decisions, challenges, and privacy review. Shows clear evolution from V1 to Final. Basic documentation present; lists steps taken but lacks reflection on why decisions were made. Documentation missing or sparse; No mention of challenges or privacy review.
Testing & Iteration Clear evidence of testing scenarios; Documented changes made based on failure/feedback. Mention of testing, but no clear changes made based on the results. No evidence that the bot was tested or improved before submission.
